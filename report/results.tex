\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

In this section we report the experimental results. We first state the
statistical protocol, then present the main test table, the comparison against
the SHARP baseline, the effect of the two training objectives, the diagnostics
that explain the negative result, and finally the error structure and
calibration of the deliverable model.

\textbf{Statistical protocol.} The held-out test sets are small (11 traces for
the primary rotation), so we treat the \emph{trace}, not the window, as the unit
of statistical analysis. Differences between configurations are assessed with a
trace-level \emph{paired} bootstrap ($N=10^4$ resamples, fixed seed): traces are
resampled with replacement and the same resample is applied to both streams, so
per-trace difficulty cancels. The bootstrap runs on \emph{accuracy}: with six of
eight classes represented by a single trace, only $\sim$2.8\% of trace resamples
contain all eight classes, so a macro-F1 bootstrap would average a different
class set in almost every replicate. Macro-F1 is therefore reported
descriptively, without an interval. Two sources of uncertainty are kept
separate: the bootstrap measures \emph{test-sampling} variance, while
\emph{training} (seed) variance is measured by the seed-43 replicates. Finally,
since $\sim$10 comparisons share the same 11 test traces, we designate C1--C2
and C1--C3 as the primary pre-registered comparisons and treat the rest as
exploratory.

\subsection{Main results}
\label{sec:res_main}

Table~\ref{tab:test} reports validation and test performance for all
configurations, with the paired accuracy difference against our model C1 and
its 95\% bootstrap interval. Three facts summarize the table:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Our model transfers best.} C1 reaches macro-F1 $0.804$ on the
held-out laboratory environment --- the highest of all configurations, and its
CE family shows by far the smallest val$\to$test degradation.
\item \textbf{Neither training objective improves over CE.} SupCon is
$7.8$ accuracy points below C1, and the interval excludes zero; the GRL variant
is $15.8$ points below, a direction stable across both seeds although the
interval is wide (see \secref{sec:res_obj}).
\item \textbf{The deep backbone beats the SHARP-style network decisively.} At
equal recipe and class set, the shallow--wide network loses $23.5$ accuracy
points, interval well away from zero (\secref{sec:res_baseline}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Our model vs.\ the SHARP baseline}
\label{sec:res_baseline}

The faithful reproduction C0 confirms the reference implementation: on the
paper's protocol (train on one bedroom subset, test on all other environments,
5 classes, decision fusion) it reaches macro-F1 $0.605$ on the unseen
environments, with per-environment accuracy between $0.50$ and $0.73$ ---
consistent with the cross-environment degradation reported in the original
work. Because C0 runs on a different split and class set, the like-for-like
architecture comparison is the ablation \emph{C1-sharplike}: the SHARP network
inserted into the exact C1 recipe (same split, classes, loss, optimizer, seed;
only the backbone differs). It reaches test macro-F1 $0.543$ against C1's
$0.804$ --- a $23.5$-point accuracy gap whose interval $[-0.399,-0.092]$
excludes zero. Interestingly, the gap on validation was $\sim$15 points and
grows to $\sim$24 on the unseen environment: depth pays off most exactly where
it matters, on cross-environment transfer.

\subsection{Do the training objectives help?}
\label{sec:res_obj}

\textbf{SupCon does not.} C3 is below C1 under every readout we tried on its
frozen features --- linear probe ($0.819$ val), NCM ($0.696$), kNN ($0.794$) ---
and on test the paired difference $-0.078$, CI $[-0.128,-0.030]$, resolves
against it. Giving SupCon a ``fair shot'' by fine-tuning the whole network from
its initialization (C3-ft) does not recover the gap either ($0.706$ on test,
$\approx$ its own linear-probe ceiling): the SupCon representation is the
limitation, not the frozen readout. A concatenation control on validation makes
the point sharper: fusing C1$\oplus$C3 features scores \emph{below} a
same-loss control C1$\oplus$C1$'$ ($0.868$ vs.\ $0.888$), so SupCon adds
nothing that a second CE encoder would not.

\textbf{The GRL costs, with high variance.} On test C2 loses $15.8$ accuracy
points to C1. The direction is stable --- it holds on both seeds (seed-43 gap:
$20.0$ points) and on validation --- but the bootstrap interval
$[-0.323,+0.034]$ crosses zero: with 11 test traces, even a 16-point gap is not
resolved at the 95\% level, and we report it as a direction with a range rather
than a point estimate. The seed replicates add a second, independent
observation: the same-config seed spread is $0.5$ accuracy points for C1 and
$4.7$ for C2 --- the adversary does not merely cost accuracy, it destabilizes
training. Finally, a linear probe on C2's \emph{frozen features} reaches $0.720$
accuracy on test, $7$ points above C2's own trained head ($0.647$): a
substantial part of the GRL damage sits in the classifier head rather than in
the representation.

\subsection{Why: the adversary has no target}
\label{sec:res_diag}

The natural question is why an environment-invariance objective fails on an
environment-transfer benchmark. We answer it by probing the \emph{frozen}
encoders directly. On a trace-disjoint inner split of the training features we
train linear probes for the activity (positive control) and for every domain
attribute, each against its own majority baseline (Table~\ref{tab:domainprobe}).
The activity control saturates, while \emph{every} domain attribute sits at or
below its majority baseline: the environment, subject and hardware attributes
are not linearly readable from the CE features at all. The same probes on the
C2 (GRL) and C3 (SupCon) encoders, and on a second rotation whose train
environments differ, give the same verdict. \fig{fig:tsne} shows the geometric
counterpart: activities form tight clusters, while capture sets are uniformly
mixed within every cluster.

The implication is structural. A GRL removes domain information that the
discriminator can read; here there is nothing to read, so the adversary can
only inject gradient noise --- which is exactly what the training monitors
showed (the domain head never rose above the majority-class floor in any GRL
run, on either seed) and what the results confirm (a cost with no invariance
gain). The root cause is the composition of the training split: one environment
dominates it, and the second is represented by a single capture set, so
environment-invariance is barely definable on this training distribution. On
this evidence C4 (SupCon+GRL) was closed without training: with no readable
domain under either loss family, its expected outcome is ``C3 plus noise''. We
report this as an evidence-based triage decision.

\subsection{Test-time adaptation and augmentation}
\label{sec:res_tta}

\textbf{TTA is neutral at best.} T3A is statistically indistinguishable from
plain C1 ($+0.007$, CI $[-0.047,+0.061]$); AdaBN \emph{hurts} ($-0.059$), and
dragging the combination down with it. Re-estimating BN statistics on a
single-environment, 11-trace unlabeled test set moves the statistics away from
a training distribution that, per the diagnostics above, was not
environment-specific to begin with --- consistent with our central finding.

\textbf{Amplitude augmentation is a regularizer, not a cross-domain lever.}
The pre-registered discriminator was: if the augmentation acts through
cross-domain invariance, its test gain should exceed its validation gain. The
opposite happened. On the primary rotation, where the baseline was already near
its ceiling, the augmentation changed nothing on validation ($-0.004$) and
slightly hurt on test ($-0.028$, CI crossing zero). On the living-room-out
rotation, whose baseline is much lower, it helped on validation ($+0.147$) and
on test by a comparable amount ($+0.073$, CI $[-0.029,+0.203]$): a gain where
there was headroom, of the same order in and out of domain --- the signature of
generic regularization on a small training set.

\subsection{Error structure and calibration of the deliverable model}
\label{sec:res_err}

\textbf{Per-class behaviour refutes a declared caveat.} The validation split of
the primary rotation contains no C, E or S traces, so checkpoint selection was
blind to three classes and we pre-declared the risk that they would underperform
on test. The opposite is true: the val-blind classes are the \emph{best} (mean
F1 $0.904$: C $0.933$, E $1.000$, S $0.780$) while the val-seen ones average
$0.744$. On the second rotation the picture flips --- a val-\emph{seen} class
collapses (S, F1 $0.131$) while the blind classes sit mid-pack. Selection
blindness is therefore not the driver of per-class error on these test sets;
class difficulty in the target environment is.

\textbf{Errors are physically sensible.} C1's dominant confusions on the
held-out environment are walking$\leftrightarrow$running (18 windows in each
direction --- adjacent Doppler signatures) and H$\to$J (14), with the remaining
mass on low-motion classes (S$\to$L/H/C); the confusion matrix is shown in
\fig{fig:confusion}. Errors between physically similar activities, rather than
scattered ones, indicate that the model transfers genuine motion features.

\textbf{Calibration.} C1 is systematically \emph{under}confident on test
(ECE $0.243$; accuracy above confidence in every reliability bin), an expected
effect of the label smoothing in its loss. The GRL model is the best calibrated
(ECE $0.070$) while being the least accurate --- calibration and accuracy
dissociate across the loss families --- and T3A, which sharpens predictions,
worsens calibration (ECE $0.345$). If a downstream application needs calibrated
confidences, temperature rescaling would be required regardless of the
configuration chosen.
