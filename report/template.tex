\begin{abstract}
WiFi Channel State Information (CSI) enables device-free human activity
recognition, but models trained in one environment degrade when deployed in
another, making cross-environment generalization the main obstacle to
practical use. We present a controlled study on the SHARP micro-Doppler
dataset under a leave-one-environment-out protocol: starting from a faithful
reproduction of the reference SHARP model, we train a deep residual network
with plain cross-entropy (CE) and ask whether two popular
representation-shaping objectives --- an adversarial domain classifier trained
through a gradient reversal layer (GRL) and a supervised contrastive (SupCon)
loss --- improve transfer to the unseen environment. All configurations share
one backbone, one data pipeline and one evaluation harness; the test set is
evaluated once, with validation-selected checkpoints, and differences are
assessed with a trace-level paired bootstrap. The deep CE model transfers
best, reaching macro-F1 $0.804$ on the held-out environment against $0.543$
for the SHARP-style shallow network in the identical recipe --- a
$23.5$-point accuracy gap resolved by the bootstrap. Neither objective helps:
SupCon loses $7.8$ accuracy points (bootstrap-resolved), and the GRL loses
$15.8$ with a $10\times$ larger observed seed spread. Frozen-encoder probes
explain why: no domain attribute is linearly readable from the CE features,
so the adversary has no target and can only inject gradient noise. Test-time
adaptation (T3A, AdaBN) does not close the gap, and an amplitude augmentation
acts as a generic regularizer rather than a cross-domain lever. Our results
suggest measuring the readability of the nuisance factor before investing in
invariance objectives, and provide a reproducible negative baseline for CSI
HAR benchmarking.
\end{abstract}
