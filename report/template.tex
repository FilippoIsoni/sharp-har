\begin{abstract}
WiFi Channel State Information (CSI) enables device-free human activity recognition, but models trained in one environment degrade when deployed in another, making cross-environment generalization the main obstacle to practical use.

We present a controlled study on the SHARP micro-Doppler dataset under a leave-one-environment-out protocol. Starting from a faithful reproduction of the reference SHARP model, we train a deep residual network with plain cross-entropy (CE) and ask whether two popular representation-shaping objectives --- an adversarial domain classifier trained through a gradient reversal layer (GRL) and a supervised contrastive (SupCon) loss --- improve transfer to the unseen environment. All configurations share one backbone, one data pipeline and one evaluation harness; the test set is evaluated once, with validation-selected checkpoints, and differences are assessed with a trace-level paired bootstrap.

Results show that the deep CE model transfers best, reaching macro-F1 $0.804$ on the held-out environment against $0.543$ for the SHARP-style shallow network in the identical recipe --- a $23.5$-point accuracy gap resolved by the bootstrap. Neither objective helps: SupCon loses $7.8$ accuracy points (significant), and the GRL loses $15.8$ with high seed instability. Frozen-encoder probes explain why: the CE features contain no linearly readable environment information, so the adversary has no target and can only add noise. Test-time adaptation is neutral (T3A) or harmful (AdaBN), and an amplitude augmentation acts as a generic regularizer rather than a cross-domain lever.

Our results suggest measuring the readability of the nuisance factor before investing in invariance objectives, and provide a reproducible negative baseline for CSI HAR benchmarking.
\end{abstract}
